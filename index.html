My name is Zachary Goldston. I am a Computer Science Student at the University of Michigan from Aurora, Ohio. I grew up being on FLL and FTC teams as the lead programmer, and found that I have an extreme interest in developing softare and learning about how to improve upon existing algorithms for computational systems.

Experiences

Michigan Mars Rover

My main activity is being the Autonomous Navigation subteam leader for Michigan Mars Rover. For our Navigation challenge at the University Rover Challenge, our rover must be able to navigate through a set of posts with AR tags on them, with the width of them decreasing by a meter as the competition progresses. My projects this past year were to develop an improved gate traversal algorithm that would produce more consistent results, by relying on the position of both posts rather than just one. Additionally, I rewrote our obstacle detection program to use the "least resistance" turn when trying to find the optimal angle to turn to when navigating an obstacle. These are just a couple of projects I have done for the Auton team. I additionally help with finances on the business team, and helped rewrite some of the team's website here. Being on Rover is instrumental in continuing to develop my interpersonal skills and teamwork with other disciplines in the engineering community. I am also able to demonstrate leadership and deal with risk management when exploring new methods and capabilities of our rover. Some of these methods are creative, out of the box ones only a group of people would be able to develop.

  

Rockwell Automation Internships

For the past two summers, and during the Fall 2020 semester, I got the chance to work for Rockwell Automation right out of high school. The first two summers I worked in the Rockwell Engineering Pathways Program in the Embedded Software department. My main projects were to work on internal systems for device comformance testing, creating developer tools to speed up information parsing, and later, working on a custom error reporting tool. During the Fall semester, I was able to continue working for them as an Embedded Software Intern, and experience day-to-day operations and meetings. The core tenants of this experience was teamwork, systems thinking, lifelong learning, and cultural awareness through seminars on race and bias hosted by the company.

  

Tau Beta Pi

This semester, I became an electee, and later the incoming Secretary, of Tau Beta Pi. I was able to meet other academically successful and forwarding thinking engineers looking to give back to the community while networking with top companies and organizations. I volunteered at several events, from tutoring sessions to K-12 learning symposiums. Through TBP, I am taught less about the technical side of things and instead focus on topics like ethics, communication, empathy, and cultural awareness.
